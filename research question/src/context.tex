According to the IEA report from March 2025, the global $\text{CO}_2$ emissions have increased by 0.8\% in 2024, reaching an all-time high of 37.8 Gt $\text{CO}_2$ a year. 
In addition, the report highlights that the energy demand grew by 2.2\% in 2024. 
This increase in $\text{CO}_2$ emissions is totally inconsistent with the goals of the Paris Agreement, 
which aims to abate the $\text{CO}_2$ emissions by 43\% by 2030 and to reach net-zero emissions by 2050.\\

In \cite{kosmadakis2024industrial}, we can read that the waste heat potential in the EU-27 and UK reached 221.32 TWh/year in 2021.
In comparision, the demand for less than 140°C heat in Germany was 612 PJ, or 170 TWh in 2012 \cite{arpagaus2018high}. 
These numbers justify the interest from companies/researchers to design and study machines 
capable of recovering this waste energy. \\

In this work, we will focus on the use of high temperature heat pumps (HTHP's) for recovering waste heat from industrial processes. 
In \cite{arpagaus2018high}, the authors provide a list of manufacturers and universities that have already been working 
on the design of several machines for a at least one decade. The already existing machines cover 
a large spectrum of applications, going from small machines of a few thousands watts to big ones of more 
than one megawatt.  
However, new design ideas can 
still be studied in order to improve the current recovery systems. \\

The main purpose of this work is to design the thermodynamical cycle of a transcritical dual-evaporator
HTHP, build it and test its performances in laboratory conditions. No similar applications were 
found in the litterature. Beside, the machine build in the frame of this master thesis should be used 
in the future for an academic purpose.
